# Getting AI to reliably analyze statutes and regulations

---

## ChatGPT gets it wrong

Almost two years ago, as a lawyer working for a major technology company with a lot of AI ambitions, I spent a lot of time working through the requirements of the then-new EU AI Act.  One day I needed to determine whether the law covered a particular situation.  Since I was experimenting with AI tools at the time, trying to understand their strengths and weaknesses, I decided to see what kind of answer I would get by feeding the entire 140+ pages of the law into ChatGPT and asking my question.  Since the length of text that ChatGPT would accept was long enough for the full text, I wondered whether it would give me a good answer.

I was surprised when it told me that the situation I was focused on was covered by the law, because having read through the law several times I was pretty sure it wasn't.  It pointed me to a section in the latter part of the law, and I quickly went to that section.  I found that... the section it pointed to had nothing to do with the issue at hand.  I pointed this out to ChatGPT, and it apologized but pointed me to another section nearby.  That section also did not have any bearing on the issue.  I went back and forth with ChatGPT, intrigued by its insistence that the issue was covered and constantly pointing to wrong (or non-existent) sections of the law.  As I had thought at the outset, the situation I was asking about was not, in fact, covered by the law.

This wasn't a big problem for me, because I had read the statute a number of times and my experimentation was not a substitute for the work I was doing.  But it seemed clear that lawyers looking for a shortcut would be tempted to take an answer from a tool like this and rely on it uncritically.  The legal news over the last few years has borne out this worry.

## Why LLMs struggle with long documents

Why did ChatGPT do this?  A simple and flippant answer is that these tools hallucinate and this is just evidence of the inherent unreliability of these tools, and for this reason they will never be useful.  I was pretty confident that this answer goes too far.  I thought this because I had already seen these tools do very useful things.  But it was clear that getting useful information from them based on a very long set of input was not reliable.  Trying out texts of different lengths confirmed that long before the input limit for ChatGPT is filled, it is likely to lose focus and fail to answer accurately about aspects of the text.  Since then, I have seen that this is a common issue with Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT.[^1]

So, is a tool like this simply not useful in helping a lawyer work through a substantial set of legal text?   Honestly, it would be easier to just throw it all aside, decide that there is nothing of value in this technology and spend my time on other things.  But the pattern of what these tools do well and do poorly made me think otherwise.  In my experience, when these tools work with smaller, more focused sets of information, they do a good job of evaluating that information and making reasonable judgments about it.  Even when they have several pages of information to analyze they do a much better job.  Based on that, I thought that there were likely ways to use these tools to take advantage of what they do well while safeguarding against what they do less well.

## Working through a document more like a lawyer does

Thinking about this, it occurred to me that the issue the AI model was running into is not altogether different from what I as a human encounter when reading a very long document.  If I read the whole thing and someone asks me a question about it, I can talk about it at a certain level with reasonable accuracy.  But I don't memorize the text and if someone asks me a very specific question, I may not immediately know the answer, and I will need to go through the document again looking for that answer.  Humans process text as we read it, retaining a certain level of knowledge of what we have read as we move through it.  If an AI model is only able to keep a limited amount of text "in focus" at one time, it may simply be dealing with a version of what humans experience.  So I started wondering about stepping an AI model through a lengthy legal document in a manner that more closely reflects the way that I work through such a document.  This project is the result of that wondering.

The theory behind this project is that by limiting the amount of input provided to the AI model in each request, and keeping it focused on limited topics, the system is designed to constrain the conditions under which hallucination can occur.  Working through a substantial amount of output as a lawyer judging the results, I have not observed hallucinations.  Where the system lacks enough information to answer a question, it records that limitation rather than fabricating an answer.  The project itself is still evolving.  I regularly encounter statutes and regulations that introduce content structures requiring additional handling.

At the outset I realized that, rather than using the chat interface for these models, I would need to write software to communicate with the AI platforms directly.  This would allow me to feed a limited amount of information to the AI models with focused instructions.  For each API call, the AI model receives only the context we expressly provide, with no memory of prior requests.  This allows the model to approach each portion of the document without the confusion that comes from trying to hold too much in mind at once.  Using the mechanisms available for such direct access (currently for OpenAI's GPT models and Anthropic's Claude models), I was able to take portions of a legal document, along with some instructions, and get a response that is reliable.

When working through a long legal document, the system I developed is able to build up a set of layered summaries of the document, at different levels of specificity and based on different portions of the overall document.  Those layers of summaries guide a later set of AI model analysts in working through the documents to find the sections that are most relevant to answering a given question.  As they work on those sections directly (not through summaries), the analysis will generally expand out to other parts of the document which are determined to be relevant as well.  In this way, the system is designed to build up an answer to a question based on the relevant text, similar to how a lawyer does.

Legal documents are particularly well suited to this approach.  The actual meaning of a law or regulation often turns on the exact definition of the terms used in the text.  Those definitions may be found in one part of the document while applying broadly; another part may elaborate on or qualify the same term for a specific set of provisions.  Provisions frequently refer to other provisions, and understanding one may require knowing what another says.  Laws and regulations also frequently cite other documents: a regulation may incorporate definitions from a parent statute, or reference requirements set out in a separate regulation.  The system maintains a registry of processed documents, allowing such cross-document references to be resolved at analysis time.  By tracking definitions, within-document cross-references, and links to other documents, the system can provide the AI model with the contextual information it needs for each portion of the document it analyzes.

The output of this process is a proposed answer that identifies the relevant provisions and cites them directly.  Those citations are grounded in the text of the document being analyzed, not in the AI model's broader training, which is what keeps them specific and checkable.  The answer is not a definitive legal opinion, and it does not resolve questions that may go beyond the documents being analyzed.  But it is a grounded starting point that a legal practitioner can use as a check on or supplement to their own analysis.

## Why open source

A process like the one described here is almost certainly being used in various legal-tech systems already on the market.  The general approach of breaking a long document into pieces, summarizing those pieces, and using the summaries to guide a question-answering process is not an original idea.  What is unavailable, when using a commercial tool, is any real visibility into the specifics of how it is done.

That matters for legal work.  A commercial tool is, from a user's perspective, largely a black box.  You can test it, but without knowing what is deterministic in the system and what is probabilistic, it is hard to reason about it.  For example, how are definitions handled in their system?  If a phrase is defined in different ways in different places in a document, each applying to different parts of the document, is there a way to look at the system to see how the definition is presented for each part?  If a section makes a reference to another section, how are those tied together?  How much text is being provided to the AI model at one time, and is there any way to know whether that is more than the model can reliably process?  What context is available to the model when evaluating each part of a document?  These are the kinds of questions that determine whether the output can be trusted for legal work, and without being able to look into the system they are often unanswerable.

An open-source system, accompanied by documentation that explains what it is doing and how, allows a technically-minded user to answer such questions.  It also allows that user, or someone they engage, to adjust the system's behavior to match the tradeoffs that matter most in a particular context.  The system logs the full inputs and outputs of every LLM call, so that anyone who wants to understand how a particular answer was reached can trace it step by step.  A log viewer tool is included for browsing these logs, including the full prompt and context provided to the model for each call.  The AI models used in this system are themselves, to some degree, black boxes.  But the system around them should be transparent.  By limiting what the models are considering at any point, and making those limits visible and adjustable, this system is designed to be transparent and open to scrutiny.  The system is also designed to fail explicitly rather than silently: if input has structural problems, processing stops with an error rather than continuing with incomplete or incorrect output.  For a tool intended for legal analysis, the difference between a visible failure and a silent one matters.

## Where to go from here

This system currently handles statutes and regulations and has not been extended to contracts or other types of legal documents.  The structure of statutes and regulations, with their explicit organization, defined terms, and cross-references, makes them a good fit for this approach, and the results have been encouraging across several bodies of law: US federal statutes, US federal regulations, California statutes, and EU regulations.

The code and documentation are available at [Cassiel Legal Workbench](https://github.com/steveyoung-random/cassiel-legal-workbench).  For readers who want a more detailed account of how the system works, including the specifics of each processing stage, [A processing pipeline for AI-assisted legal document analysis](PIPELINE_OVERVIEW.md) covers that ground.  A technically-minded practitioner, or someone they can engage for the purpose, can use the documentation to examine the system's design choices, verify its behavior on a specific document, or modify it for a particular use case.
